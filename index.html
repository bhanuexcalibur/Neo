01The Manifesto, Sharpened

Neo already named the five problems. Here’s the sentence a buyer would actually say.

Neo’s own manifesto page lays out a sharp internal thesis: context fragmentation, process knowledge that lives only in people’s heads, adoption inertia, integration tax, and compliance and cost opacity. It’s a good engineering read. It’s not yet something a VP of Ops would recognize as their own Tuesday. Here’s the outward line for each one.

As written: enterprise knowledge is fragmented across email, chat, docs, and tools, so AI only ever sees a slice of the picture.
“Your AI assistant writes beautifully. It just has no idea your Q3 numbers live in a Slack thread from March.”
As written: skills, SOPs, and institutional know-how live in people’s memories, not in a format AI can execute against.
“The person who actually knows how you close this kind of deal is one resignation letter away from taking that knowledge with them.”
As written: there’s no shared skill library, so what works on one team never reaches another, and every person rebuilds from zero.
“Someone on your ops team already built the perfect AI workflow for this exact task. Nobody else on the team knows it exists.”
As written: connecting AI to the dozens of tools an enterprise actually runs on costs more effort than the AI work itself.
“You didn’t buy an AI seat to spend the first quarter wiring it to your CRM.”
As written: enterprises need to monitor AI usage, data access, decisions, and cost in one place.
“Ask your CFO if they can tell you what your AI tools actually did last month. Most can’t answer that yet.”

Source: neo.work/manifesto, paraphrased from Neo’s own published framing. The five customer lines are my rewrite, not Neo copy.

02Category

The category name matters more than usual here.

Neo isn’t launching into empty space. Coverage of the launch already frames it against Microsoft Copilot, Google Gemini for Workspace, Notion AI, Slack AI, ClickUp, Asana, and Atlassian. Every one of those has a foothold and a limitation, and the honest version of that comparison is more convincing than pretending the category is empty.

Tool
What it’s actually for
Where Neo’s bet is different
MMicrosoft Copilot
An AI layer inside Office and Teams. Strong where a company’s working context already lives in Microsoft 365.
Copilot goes where Microsoft already sits. Neo’s bet is that most companies’ real context lives outside Microsoft too, in Slack threads, Salesforce records, and Notion docs.
NNotion AI
A capable writing and Q&A layer over Notion’s own docs and wikis.
Strong for what’s already written down inside Notion. No native task delegation layer, and no agent that executes work across other tools.
AAsana / ClickUp
Mature project management tools, now shipping their own AI feature layers on top.
Task tracking is the starting point here, not the end state. Neo treats task management as one surface of four, with Friday as the execution layer across all of them, not a bolt-on.
SSlack AI
Summarizes and searches what already happened inside Slack.
Sees one channel of context, well. Neo’s pitch is that no single tool, Slack included, holds the whole picture alone.
The honest read: none of these are the wrong tool. Each one owns a room in the building well. Neo’s bet is that the value nobody’s captured yet sits in the hallway connecting them.

Competitor framing is based on public product positioning and press coverage of Neo’s launch, not a formal battlecard from Neo. I haven’t seen an internal comp doc.

03Two Launches

The habit being launched isn’t a feature. It’s delegation.

Reported timeline: select access around August 2026, wider release around January 2027, both from press coverage, not Neo’s own site. The real risk in that window isn’t awareness. It’s the first time someone has to actually trust Friday with a task instead of doing it themselves.

Now

Guarantee the first delegation

  • One pre-built task in onboarding that can’t fail: a renewal, a follow-up, a report
  • Day one becomes a completed handoff, not an empty inbox waiting for input
Select access

Name the moment, not the waitlist

  • Track time-to-first-delegation per account as the real activation metric
  • Pre-seed 5 to 8 accounts willing to be named once they hit it
Toward Jan 2027

Publish delegation, not testimonials

  • “She delegated her renewal, her onboarding doc, her weekly report”, told in Neo’s own words
  • Lock the category table from Section 02 before competitors define it first
Success metrics The four numbers that matter more than waitlist size
Speed

Time to first completed delegation, per account

Depth

Spread

05Running the Team

Month one, running the function, not just the launch.

A marketing team pitching “adoption inertia” while its own campaigns restart from zero every time isn’t a good look. Here’s how I’d avoid becoming the case study Neo is trying to fix.

Week one

One positioning doc, not five decks

A single source of truth for messaging and the comparison table from Section 02, so every deck and campaign pulls from the same sentence instead of drifting.

Ongoing

A weekly, AI-assisted category scan

Copilot, Gemini for Workspace, and Notion AI ship fast. A standing weekly pass on what changed keeps the comparison table current instead of stale by the time it’s used.

Team workflow

A shared prompt and template library

Adoption inertia, fixed inside the team itself: when someone builds a good workflow for a brief or a battlecard, it goes into a shared library, not their own head.
